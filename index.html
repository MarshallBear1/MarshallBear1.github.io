Description
The current count of lncRNAs stands at approximately 130,000. The conventional definition categorizes any gene of 200 nucleotides or more as an lncRNA, leading to a vast assortment of genes falling under a single classification. We aim to refine this broad definition by introducing a new nomenclature based on three key characteristics. This approach will allow a thorough understanding of an lncRNA's characteristics just from its name. By interacting with the boxes, you will see a detailed breakdown of these three categories. To verify your understanding, we also offer a quiz that tests your comprehension of this nomenclature.


Subclasses:



Intergenic lncRNAs are transcribed from regions of the genome that do not overlap with protein-coding genes. They have diverse functions and play important roles in gene regulation and chromatin organization.

Intronic lncRNAs are transcribed from intronic regions of protein-coding genes. They can regulate gene expression by interacting with chromatin and modulating transcription.

Antisense lncRNAs are transcribed from the opposite strand of protein-coding genes. They can regulate gene expression through various mechanisms, such as transcriptional interference and RNA-RNA interactions.

Sense lncRNAs are transcribed from the same strand as protein-coding genes. They can regulate gene expression at multiple levels, including transcriptional and post-transcriptional regulation.

Sense Overlapping lncRNAs are transcribed from the same strand and overlapping regions of protein-coding genes.

Divergent lncRNAs are transcribed in the opposite direction to a nearby protein-coding gene, from a shared promoter region. They often share regulatory elements and expression patterns with the adjacent gene.

lncRNAs that overlap with the 3' untranslated region (UTR) of protein-coding genes. They can regulate mRNA stability, translation, and localization.

lncRNAs that overlap with the 5' untranslated region (UTR) of protein-coding genes. They can regulate transcription, translation, and mRNA stability.

Very long intergenic non-coding RNAs (vlincRNAs) are a subset of lincRNAs that are exceptionally long (> 10 kb) and often expressed at low levels. They may have unique biological functions.

Pseudogene RNAs are non-functional copies of protein-coding genes that have lost their protein-coding potential. Some pseudogene RNAs can have regulatory functions.

Circular RNAs (circRNAs) are formed by back-splicing of pre-mRNA molecules, resulting in a covalently closed loop structure. They are highly stable and have been implicated in various biological processes.

Enhancer RNAs (eRNAs) are transcribed from enhancer regions of the genome. They play a role in enhancer-promoter communication and gene regulation.

Natural antisense transcripts (NATs) are RNAs that are transcribed from the opposite strand of protein-coding genes. They can regulate gene expression through various mechanisms, such as transcriptional interference and RNA-RNA interactions.

Antisense to ribosomal RNA (arcRNA) are non-coding RNAs that are transcribed from the antisense strand of ribosomal RNA genes. They have been implicated in ribosome biogenesis and gene regulation.

Highly overlapping lincRNAs (hovlincRNAs) are a group of lincRNAs that show extensive overlap with protein-coding genes. They may have functional interactions with the overlapping genes.

Competing endogenous RNAs (ceRNAs) are RNAs that compete for shared microRNA binding sites, thereby regulating each other's expression. They play a role in gene regulatory networks.

cis-regulatory RNAs (cisRNAs) are RNAs that are transcribed from regions adjacent to their target genes. They can regulate gene expression in a cis-regulatory manner.

trans-regulatory RNAs (transRNAs) are RNAs that are transcribed from regions distal to their target genes. They can regulate gene expression in a trans-regulatory manner.

Other structures of lncRNAs that do not fall into the defined categories. They have diverse functions and may represent novel classes of lncRNAs.
